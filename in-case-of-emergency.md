              <li>
                Myoclonus is the term used to describe the sudden, involuntary jerking of a muscle or group of muscles caused by muscle contractions (positive
                myoclonus) or muscle relaxation (negative myoclonus).&nbsp; The twitching or jerking of muscles cannot be controlled by the person experiencing
                it.&nbsp; Myoclonic jerks may occur infrequently or many times a minute.&nbsp; They sometimes occur in response to an external event or when a
                person attempts to make a movement.&nbsp; By itself, myoclonus may be seen as a symptom rather than a disease.&nbsp; To some degree, it may
                occur occasionally to otherwise healthy people.&nbsp; (For instance, hiccups may be considered a type of myoclonus.)&nbsp; In severe cases, it
                can interfere with movement control and balance, and limit various everyday activities such as eating or talking.

            Overview
            <ul>
              <li>
                Posttraumatic stress disorder (PTSD), once called shell shock or battle fatigue syndrome, is a serious condition that can develop after a person
                has experienced or witnessed a traumatic or terrifying event in which serious physical harm occurred or was threatened.&nbsp; PTSD is a lasting
                consequence of traumatic ordeals that cause intense fear, helplessness, or horror, such as a sexual or physical assault, the unexpected death of
                a loved one, an accident, war, or natural disaster.&nbsp; Families of victims can also develop PTSD, as can emergency personnel and rescue
                workers.<br />
                &nbsp;<br />
                Most people who experience a traumatic event will have reactions that may include shock, anger, nervousness, fear, and even guilt.&nbsp; These
                reactions are common, and for most people, they go away over time.&nbsp; For a person with PTSD, however, these feelings continue and even
                increase, becoming so strong that they keep the person from living a normal life.&nbsp; People with PTSD have symptoms for longer than one month
                and cannot function as well as before the event occurred.
              </li>

        <ul>
          <li>
            Overview
            <ul>
              <li>
                Glaucoma results from damage to the optic nerve.&nbsp; Usually the damage is from increased pressure in the eye.&nbsp; The damage to the eye is
                irreversible and glaucoma can lead to blindness.&nbsp; The most common form, open-angle glaucoma, generally appears in middle age and seems to
                have a genetic component.&nbsp; In this type of glaucoma, vision loss occurs very gradually.&nbsp; One eye is often worse than the other.&nbsp;
                Other types of glaucoma are closed-angle glaucoma (a medical emergency) and congenital glaucoma (present at birth).&nbsp; Secondary glaucoma is
                usually associated with another eye disease or disorder, such as a very mature cataract, uveitis, bleeding, eye tumor, or an eye injury.
              </li>
